Making Videos Accessible

Accessibility refers to the practice of making websites usable for all people, including those with disabilities, ensuring that everyone has equal access to content. In this guide, we focus on how to achieve this for embedded videos.

Why Make Videos Accessible

Why make your videos accessible? Before jumping into the details of video accessibility, let's first address the question of why. To some, it may seem like additional work with a minimal payoff (i.e. targeting only a small audience). However, making a video accessible is less time-consuming or expensive than you may think. Additional benefits also exist. For example, tagging your video clip with appropriate metadata is useful for SEO as well as for captioning.

Your audience is larger than you may think. Video accessibility benefits many different groups of users:

  • Users with disabilities (hearing or visually impaired).
  • Users with partial disabilities (moderate to severe visual and hearing limitations).
  • Users who are watching video in a foreign language (international viewers).
  • Circumstances when video playback itself is impaired (audio playback is forbidden or broken)
  • Moments when users are unable to watch the screen (watching video while driving)

Google, your main SEO-driver, requires this same meta-data to better index your content in it search engines. Thus, making your videos accessible will absolutely help Google find and index them. Your small effort then has big payoff: more and better targeted visitors to your site.

The last piece in deciding whether or not to make your videos accessible are government-based requirements. In many countries, providing accessible video is required for federal agencies and companies working with/for them. In the United States, this is covered by the Section 508 Amendment to the Rehabilitation Act. In many other countries (like the Netherlands), the W3C Web Content Accessibility Guidelines are used for similar legislation.

Accessibility Guidelines

So what does it take to make a video accessible? The raw guidelines (WCAG/508) are quite elaborate and can be technical), but here's what you generally need:

Progressive Enhancement
You should not assume that all of your visitors have certain plugins (Flash) or browser features (HTML5) available. Instead, start out with a basic HTML setup (video download link with text description) and then enhance this with scripting and plugins as these features are detected.
No Autostart
Videos should not automatically start upon page load. This is frustrating to every visitor, but especially challenging to those who don't see or hear the video. These users have to use the keyboard to navigate through the page, find the videoplayer and then find the pause button.
Keyboard Controls
Videos should also be controllable by the keyboard (play/pause, seeking, volume, etc.). Essentially, you should guarantee that a user can "tab" through the controlbar. Hooking up custom keyboard controls less useful, since these are non-standardized and may break existing accessibility/access-key integrations.
Closed Captions
Captions are very similar to subtitles, but instead of just speech, all relevant audio is described (e.g. "the phone is ringing"). Captions are "closed" when they can be toggled on/off during playback. The alternative, captions burned into the video, is called "open". Making captions closed is important, because it allows you to serve everybody with the same setup (inclusiveness).
Audio Description
An audio description is a second audio track with a voiceover that can be turned on/off. Just like captions describe the audio, an audio description describes all relevant visual information. Voiceovers are placed into "natural silences", so they don't interfere with the original audio. Audio descriptions are not trivial to produce and not always needed (e.g. not with interviews).

The full list of guidelines only applies to on-demand video under the WCAG regulations. Other regulations, like Section 508 or the FCC Regulations, do not require audio descriptions. For live video, audio descriptions are also never needed.

For audio (podcasts), captions are also not required. Instead, a separate transcript (text version) of the audio file will suffice. In practice (workflow wise), it is often easier to use captions for audio clips too.

An Example Setup

Let's now look at an example setup of an accessible video. Click the image to see the example in action:

Here is the embed code of this example:

<p id="container">
    <a href="/upload/corrie.mp4">The video clip from ITV's Coronation Street</a> (MP4, 00:45)
</p>

<script type="text/javascript">
    jwplayer("container").setup({
        autostart: false,
        height: 360,
        playlist: [{
            captions: [{
                '/assets/corrie.srt'
            }],
            image: '/assets/corrie.jpg',
            sources: [{
                file: '/assets/corrie.mp4'
            }]
        }],
        width: 640
    });
</script>

This embed code has the following accessibility features:

  • Per the guideline on Progressive Enhancement, the clip is first presented as a basic download link. This download is in MP4 format, using H264 video and AAC audio. MP4 is the preferred option, supported by nearly every browser, device and media player today.
  • Next, JavaScript is used to embed JW Player over the download link. This happens when JavaScript is enabled and HTML5 or Flash is detected:
  • Autostarting is disabled, so the second guideline is met. Instead, a preview image is displayed to trigger users to start the video.
  • It is possible for users to tab through buttons in JW Player to control them by keyboard. This is a built-in feature of JW Player that works in both Flash and HTML5.
  • Finally, Closed Captions are loaded as an SRT file into the player, using the captions config block. You can use any text editor to author the captions; cool dedicated tools Subtitle Workshop (desktop) or Subtitle Horse (online).

Note the example does not contain an audio description. JW Player 6 does not yet support audio descriptions (see below).

Some Considerations

As with any real-world implementation, the above example is a simple suggestion for an accessible video. Here are some other things to keep in mind:

  • The JW Player provides keyboard access to all of its buttons (pause, mute, fullscreen, captions, audiodescription), but not to its sliders yet. We still have to incorporate slider control, following the ARIA guidelines.
  • When Flash is used for rendering, it is not possible to tab in or tab out of the player using your keyboard. This is a major issue, since it essentially breaks keyboard access. Luckily, screen readers like JAWS do provide the option to enter/exit Flash elements. In HTML5, this problem does not exist.
  • On mobile devices, captions cannot yet be rendered by JW Player. This because there's no way to display anything on top of (full-screen) video on these devices. There is a workaround for iOS, using embedded TX3G text tracks.
  • Audio descriptions are not yet supported on browsers and devices, but coming as part of the new HTML5 <track> element. For now, it is best to create two versions of your videos: one with and one without descriptions in the audio. Users can then select which one to play.

About <track>

Work is in progress to build both captions and audio descriptions into HTML5. This is done through a new element: <track>. Though not built into any real-world browser yet, the spec is mostly done. This is how it will look:

<video controls height="360" src="/assets/corrie.mp4" width="640">
    <track label="Closed Captions" kind="captions" src="/assets/corrie-cc.vtt" />
    <track label="Audio Description" kind="descriptions" src="/assets/bunny-ad.vtt" />
</video>

Browsers and devices will see the tracks, pick up the VTT files and render controls to toggle them on/off. VTT files are basically extended SRT files, so if you provide your captions in SRT today, you can easily switch to VTT in the future.

Important to note is that the audio description is also a VTT text file. The browser (or screen reader) is supposed to load this file and provide text-to-speech synthesis. If this actually gets supported, the creation of audio descriptions will become easy and affordable.
